**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #the #quot #of #lt #gt

The name Sangpyeong is a shortened version of sang si pyeong joon, meaning &quot;a consistent value at all times and easily usable by anyone.&quot; On the obverse of the coin, each letter of the word &quot;sang, pyeong,&quot; (vertical) and &quot;tong, bo&quot; (horizontal) was stamped around a rectangular hole in the middle, while the top of the reverse showed an abbreviation for the name of the mint. The Thousand-Character Classic, numeral letters, or codes were additionally printed later to stem counterfeiting and apportion blame. According to the unit of old Korean coinage, one Sangpyeong Tongbo equaled one Moon. One hundred Moon equaled ten Jeon, and ten Jeon equaled one Nyang.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

## 08:17 — Banliang
**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #the #of #lt #gt #in
**url**: https://www.bok.or.kr/portal/bbs/B0000338/view.do?nttId=10085240&menuNo=200761

&lt;p&gt;&lt;span style=&quot;font-size: 10pt; font-family: &#39;맑은 고딕&#39;;&quot;&gt;&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;The ban liang coin was first issued in the Qin dynasty during the reign of China&amp;rsquo;s first emperor, Qin Shi Huang, who unified China&#39;s politically warring states in 221 BC and their currencies in an effort to lay secure foundations for a new centralized state. The coin was round with a square hole in the middle, reflecting the traditional cosmology of East Asia in which the sky is round and the earth square. Thereafter, the shape of the ban liang coin became the standard form for other coins made across Korea, Japan and Vietnam, as well as in China in the following periods. The coin took its name from the two-character inscription, ban liang (&lt;/span&gt;半兩&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;), in seal script. The liang is an ancient unit of weight consisting of 24 zhu (or su), each of which is equivalent to about the weight of 100 grains of black millet, approximately 0.65 grams, and, accordingly, a ban (&amp;ldquo;half&amp;rdquo;) liang consists of 12 zhu, or approximately 7.8 grams.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

## 08:17 — Kanei Tsuho
**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #lt #gt #the #in #span
**url**: https://www.bok.or.kr/portal/bbs/B0000338/view.do?nttId=10085236&menuNo=200761

&lt;p&gt;&lt;span style=&quot;font-size: 10pt; font-family: &#39;맑은 고딕&#39;;&quot;&gt;&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;The Kanei Tsuho is a Japanese coin that is comparable to Joseon&amp;rsquo;s Sangpyeong Tongbo in that it was issued in larger amounts and in more types than any other coin, and was circulated for the longest period in the history of Japanese currency. The coin was issued in local mints across Japan for about 240 years from 1626, the 3rd Kanei year in the Tokugawa Shogunate period, to 1868 and was used widely by the common people of Japan. The front face of the coin is inscribed with four Chinese characters representing its name, Kanei Tsuho (&lt;/span&gt;寬永通寶&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;), while its rear face contains inscriptions of the name of the area, a decorative design, numbers and characters.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

## 08:17 — Daming Tongxing Baochao
**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #the #lt #gt #of #span
**url**: https://www.bok.or.kr/portal/bbs/B0000338/view.do?nttId=10085235&menuNo=200761

&lt;p&gt;&lt;span style=&quot;font-size: 10pt; font-family: &#39;맑은 고딕&#39;;&quot;&gt;&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;The Daming Tongxing Baochao is a paper note that was circulated in Ming China during the fourteenth century, and is known to have been the world&#39;s largest banknote. Issued during the Hongwu period (1328 &amp;ndash; 1398), this particular banknote contains the inscription &amp;ldquo;Daming Tongxing Baochao&amp;rdquo; (&lt;/span&gt;大明通行寶鈔&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;) written in regular script at the top of its front face, and eight characters, &amp;ldquo;Daming Baochao Tianxia Tongxing&amp;rdquo; (&lt;/span&gt;大明寶鈔天下通行&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;) written in seal script on the upper part of both faces. The center of the note shows the denomination of 1 gwan, with a design of ten bunches of coins placed directly beneath it to help illiterate users understand the function and value of the note. The lower part of the note contains a warning to the effect that anyone who forged this note would be subject to severe punishment.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

## 08:17 — Dangbaekjeon
**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #lt #gt #the #span #quot
**url**: https://www.bok.or.kr/portal/bbs/B0000338/view.do?nttId=10085232&menuNo=200761

&lt;p&gt;&lt;span style=&quot;font-size: 10pt; font-family: &#39;맑은 고딕&#39;;&quot;&gt;&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;In November 1866, the third year of the reign of King Gojong of the Joseon Dynasty, the government issued Dangbaekjeon coins, which came to be used nationwide for about six months to raise additional funds through seigniorage. On the obverse of the coin printed Sangpyeong Tongbo (&lt;/span&gt;常平通寶&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;), while the reverse showed Hodaedangback (&lt;/span&gt;戶大當百&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;). Dangbaekjeon coins weighed only five to six times than Sangpyeong Tongbo coins, but its denomination was around 100 times that of the Sangpyeong Tongbo coins. The government stopped its minting in May 1867 since this high-value coin, which increased only face value, caused rapid inflation.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

## 08:17 — Byeoljeon
**source**: bok-press · **tags**: #lt #gt #the #coins #of
**url**: https://www.bok.or.kr/portal/bbs/B0000338/view.do?nttId=10085231&menuNo=200761

&lt;p&gt;&lt;span style=&quot;font-size: 10pt; font-family: &#39;맑은 고딕&#39;;&quot;&gt;&lt;span lang=&quot;EN-US&quot;&gt;Byeoljeon coins were minted in the reign of King Sukjong of late Joseon. The coin was initially produced to check the quality and weight of materials used for Sangpyeong Tongbo coins, but it also served as a commemorative coin with sophisticated decorations and strong symbolization. The royal family or the gentry mainly owned these coins. At the end of the Joseon Dynasty, key charms bundling up several Byeoljeon coins became articles for marriage among the upper class. Byeoljeon coins with Norigae, Korean traditional ornaments, or keys dangled meant good luck.&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;br&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
